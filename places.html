Places to See

Iconic landmarks, scenic roads, and natural wonders of the valley

Iten

Perched on the rim of the Elgeyo Escarpment, Iten is Kenya's most famous training town. Sunrise runs along red-dirt tracks, cold highland air, and a view across the entire rift floor make this a bucket-list destination in its own right.

Visit the High Altitude Training Centre, watch elite athletes train at dawn, and enjoy the unforgettable escarpment viewpoint that drops sheer into the valley below.

Biretwo Road

The road from Iten down to the valley floor via Biretwo is one of Kenya's most spectacular drives. It winds through dense forest, past terraced shamba farms, and explodes into panoramic views at every hairpin bend.

The descent drops nearly 1,000 metres over roughly 20 km, and the temperature rises with every kilometre. The road is beloved by cyclists who climb it as one of Africa's great cycling challenges.

Plan a Visit

Chebloch Bridge

Chebloch Bridge spans the Kerio River at the narrowest and deepest section of the gorge. Standing on the bridge, sheer cliffs rise on both sides while crocodiles rest on the sandy banks far below.

The bridge is the main crossing point between Elgeyo-Marakwet and Baringo counties, and the dramatic landscape around it has made it one of the valley's most photographed spots.

Plan a Visit

Kerio River

The Kerio River flows the length of the valley, carving the gorge that gives the whole region its name. Its banks shelter Nile crocodiles, hippos (in the lower reaches), olive baboons, and a remarkable diversity of birdlife.

The river is also the source of life for valley communities — irrigation furrows, fish, and fresh water all flow from this single watercourse that has shaped human settlement here for thousands of years.

More to Discover

Tambach

The bustling gateway town at the base of the escarpment — a great base for valley exploration with local markets and guesthouses.

Arror River & Dam

The Arror joins the Kerio from the north, forming rich wetlands and a dam that supports both wildlife and local fishing communities.

Marakwet Irrigation Furrows

500-year-old channels carved into the escarpment by the Marakwet people — a UNESCO-recognised feat of traditional engineering still in use.
