Culture & People

The communities, traditions, and living heritage of the Kerio Valley

People of the Rift

The Kerio Valley is home to three distinct communities who have shaped this landscape for centuries — the Elgeyo (Keiyo) on the escarpment highlands, the Marakwet to the north along the cliff tops, and the Tugen across the valley on the Baringo side.

Each community developed a remarkable adaptation to the dramatic terrain: the Marakwet carved irrigation furrows into sheer rock faces, the Elgeyo built homesteads on cliff edges, and the Tugen developed pastoralism on the hot valley floor. Together they created one of Kenya's richest cultural landscapes — and they still live it today.

Three Communities, One Valley

Each community brings its own traditions, language, and relationship with the land.

The Elgeyo (Keiyo)

The Elgeyo people inhabit the highland plateau and escarpment around Iten. Known for their athletic traditions long before the world took notice, they have produced many of Kenya's greatest distance runners. Their homesteads command the escarpment rim with views across the entire valley.

The Marakwet

The Marakwet occupy the northern escarpment and are celebrated for their ancient irrigation system — a network of furrows (called aror) carved into cliff faces that channels water from highland rivers to farms hundreds of metres below. This system, still in daily use, is recognised as a feat of traditional engineering.

The Tugen

The Tugen live on the eastern side of the valley — the Tugen Hills above and the hot valley floor below. Traditionally pastoralists, they are renowned for their elaborate age-set ceremonies, cattle culture, and oral history traditions that stretch back thousands of years.

Ceremonies & Traditions

All three communities share a deep tradition of age-set organisation — a social structure in which men and women pass through defined life stages (initiation, warrior hood, elder hood) each marked by ceremony, song, and communal celebration.

Initiation ceremonies (called tumdo in Keiyo) mark the transition from youth to adulthood and are among the most important events in community life. They involve days of singing, dancing, teaching, and feasting — and bind age-mates together for life.

Traditional music features the sebet (a stringed instrument), drums, and communal call-and-response singing. At festivals you will hear songs composed generations ago, still taught and performed in exactly the same way.
